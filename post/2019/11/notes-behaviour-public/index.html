Behavioural Public Policy

Departures from the standard model:

  • Present bias: can not correctly understand future self
  • Salience bias: can only notice the most obvious thing
  • Non-selfish preferences: contribute to public goods

which lead to:

  • Create scope for policy intervention (?)
  • Worsen market failures
  • Reduce market failures

Present Bias

Theory: Intertemporal Consumption

  • Utility discounting: \[U = u(c_t) + \beta \big[\delta u(c_{t+1}) + \delta^2 u(c_{t+2}) + \delta^3 u(c_{t+3}) + \cdots + \delta^T u(c_{t+T})\big]\]
    • Standard model: \(\beta=1\)
    • Present bias: \(\beta<1\)
  • Budget constraint (can borrow): \[c_t + c_{t+1} + \dots + c_{t+T} \leq y_t + y_{t+1} + \dots + y_{t+T}\]
    • Total available assets: \[\begin{aligned}a_t &= y_t + y_{t+1} + \dots + y_{t+T} \\ a_{t+1} &= a_t - c_t\end{aligned}\]
  • Bellman equation: for all \(s = t, t+1,\dots, t+T\) \[\begin{aligned}V_t(a_t) &= \max_{c_t} \ u(c_t) + \delta\cdot V_{t+1}(a_t - c_t) \\ &= \max_{c_t} \ u(c_t) + \delta\cdot \Big[ \underbrace{ u\big(c_{t+1}(a_t - c_t)\big) +\delta\cdot V_{t+2}\big(a_t - c_t - c_{t+1}(a_t - c_t)\big)}_{V_{t+1}(a_t - c_t)}\Big]\end{aligned}\] Therefore, FOC: \[\begin{aligned}u'(c_s) &= \delta \cdot V'_{s+1}(a_s - c_s) \\ &= \delta \cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a} \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+\delta^2\cdot V'_{s+2}\big(a_t - c_t - c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \Big(1-\frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a}\Big)\\ u'(c_{s+1}) &= \delta\cdot V'_{s+2}(a_{s+1} - c_{s+1}) \\ u'(c_s) &= \delta\cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a} \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+ \delta\cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \big(1 -\frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a}\big)\\ &= \delta\cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \end{aligned}\] \(\implies\) Euler equation

Agents

  • TC
    • \(\beta=1\)
    • Set consumption s.t. \[u'(c_t^{TC}) = \delta\cdot u'(c_{t+1}^{TC})\]
    • (In standard model:) \[u'(c_t^{TC}) = \frac{p_t}{p_{t+1}}\cdot u'(c_{t+1}^{TC})\]
  • Naif
    • \(\beta <1\), but think themselves will behave like TC in the future \(\implies\) over-consumption in this period
    • Set consumption s.t. \[u'(c_t^{N}) = \beta\delta\cdot u'(c_{t+1}^{N})\]
  • Sophisticate
    • \(\beta <1\), and they know it
    • Set consumption s.t. \[u'(c_t^S) = u'(c_{t+1}^S)\cdot\Big(\beta\delta\cdot\frac{\partial c_{t+1}^S}{\partial a}+\delta\big(1-\frac{\partial c_{t+1}^S}{\partial a}\big)\Big)\]
    • If \(\frac{\partial c_{t+1}^S}{\partial a}\approx 1\), no motive to save
    • If \(\frac{\partial c_{t+1}^S}{\partial a}\ll 1\), save & more patient
  • Naif vs. Sophisticate
    • Naif’s false optimism help to reduce current consumption
    • Sophisticate’s correct self assessment help reduce current consumption

Iso-Elastic Utility

  • Utility function: \[u(c) = \frac{1}{1-\sigma}\cdot c^{1-\sigma}\]
  • TC’s consumption: \[\Delta \log c_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot\Delta\log p_t + \frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot\log\delta\] Therefore, monthly consumption: \[\log c_{t+30} - \log c_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot(\log p_{t+30} -\log p_t) + 30\cdot\frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot\log\delta\] if price does not change: \[\log c_{t+30} - \log c_t \approx 30\cdot\frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot\log\delta<0\]

Lab Experiment

  • Procrastination & present bias in work
  • Tedious work for two sessions
    • \(t=0\): initial allocation: \(e_1\) and \(e_2\)
    • \(t=1\): re-allocation; randomly pick between the allocations; session 1
    • \(t=2\): session 2
  • Notations
    • \(R\): interest rate
    • \(B\): basic income
    • \(e_1\) and \(e_2\): effort costs
    • \(e_1 + R e_2 = 50\): budget constraint
  • \(t=0\) \[\begin{aligned}\max_{e_1^0,e_2^0} && U_0 &= \beta\delta^2 B - \beta (\delta c(e_1) + \delta^2 c(e_2))\\ FOC && c'(e_1) &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2)\end{aligned}\]
    • \(\frac{\delta}{R}=1\implies e_1=e_2\)
    • \(\frac{\delta}{R}<1\implies e_1<e_2\)
    • \(\frac{\delta}{R}>1\implies e_1>e_2\)
  • \(t=1\) \[\begin{aligned}\max_{e_1^1,e_2^1} && U_1 &= \beta\delta B - (c(e_1) + \beta\delta c(e_2))\\ FOC && c'(e_1) &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2) < \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2)\end{aligned}\]
    • \(\implies e_1^1<e_1^0\), \(e_2^1>e_2^0\)
    • 5% - 10% procrastination
    • Is that a lot?

Calibration (Specifying the Model by Guessing)

  • Assume \(c(e)=0.5\cdot c\cdot e^2\)
    • \(c'(e) = ce\)
  • When \(t=0\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^0) &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^0)\\ ce_1^0 &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot ce_2^0 \\ \implies \delta &= R\cdot\frac{e_1^0}{e_2^0}\end{aligned}\]
  • When \(t=1\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^1) &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^1) \\ ce_1^1 &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot ce_2^1 \\ \implies \beta\delta &= R\cdot\frac{e_1^1}{e_2^1}\end{aligned}\]
  • Therefore, present bias: \[\beta = \frac{e_1^1\ /\ e_2^1}{e_1^0\ /\ e_2^0}\]

Structural Estimation (Better Guessing with Generalisation)

  • Assume \(c(e)=\frac{1}{\gamma}\cdot c\cdot e^{\gamma}\)
    • \(\gamma\geq 1\)
  • When \(t=0\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^0) &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^0)\\ c(e_1^0)^{\gamma -1} &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c(e_2^0)^{\gamma -1} \end{aligned}\] take log of both sides: \[\begin{aligned}(\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_1^0 &= \log \delta - \log R + (\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_2^0 \\ \log e_2^0 - \log e_1^0 &= \frac{1}{\gamma -1}(- \log \delta + \log R) \end{aligned}\]
  • When \(t=1\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^1) &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^1) \\ c(e_1^1)^{\gamma -1} &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c(e_2^1)^{\gamma -1} \end{aligned}\] take log of both sides: \[\begin{aligned}(\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_1^1 &= \log \beta + \log \delta - \log R + (\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_2^1 \\ \log e_2^1 - \log e_1^1 &= \frac{1}{\gamma -1}(- \log \beta - \log \delta + \log R )\end{aligned}\]
  • \(\implies\) Econometrics model: \[\log e_{2ik}^t - \log e_{1ik}^t = \underbrace{\phi_0}_{\frac{-\log \delta}{\gamma -1}} +\underbrace{\phi_1}_{\frac{-\log \beta}{\gamma -1}}\cdot 1(t=1) +\underbrace{\phi_2}_{\frac{1}{\gamma -1}}\cdot \log R_k + \epsilon_{ikt}\]
    • \(k\): different values of \(R\)
    • \(\phi_2\): elasticity of effort wrt \(R\)
    • \(\delta = \exp\big(-\frac{\phi_0}{\phi_2}\big)\)
    • \(\beta = \exp\big(-\frac{\phi_1}{\phi_2}\big)\)

Food Stamp: Shapiro (2005)

Experiment

  • Measure actual (daily) consumption, not monetary expenditure
  • Caloric intakes decreases by 0.45% each day after receiving food stamp

Explaining Using Standard Model

  • Assume \(\sigma=1\), \(\implies\delta=\exp(-0.0045)\approx 0.995\),
    which makes yearly discount factor \(\delta^{365}=0.16\) (too low!)
  • Assume \(\delta^{365}=0.95\), therefore elasticity: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\log\delta}{\sigma} &= -0.0045 \\ \frac{1}{\sigma} &= \frac{-0.0045}{\log 0.95^{1/365}} \\ &= 32.02\end{aligned}\] \(\implies\) 1% change in price cause 32% decrease in consumption (too large!)

Behavioural Model

  • The value of calorie for each day is different:
    • Running out of money
    • Have to skip a meal because running out of money
  • Assume sophisticate, \(\beta=0.96\)
  • Assume naif \(\beta\) is even closer to 1

Firm 1

Optimal Taxation (Benchmark)

  • Price-taking consumers
    • Utility: \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(x_1,x_2) + y \\ &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) x_2\cdot p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • Budget: \[\begin{aligned}y &= z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\\ z &\geq (p_1+t)\cdot x_1 + p_2x_2 \end{aligned}\]
    • Demand (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}x_1^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_1 &= p_1 + t \\ x_2^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • \(y\): \(p=1\), no income effect (all money left)
    • \(t\): tax rate (assume that consumers are nominal tax payer)
  • Price-taking firms
    • Profit function: \[\pi = p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 - c(x_1, x_2)\]
    • Supply (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}x_1^S(p_1,p_2): && c_1 &= p_1 \\ x_2^S(p_1,p_2): && c_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Market clearance: \[\begin{cases}x_1^D = x_1^S \\ x_2^D = x_2^S \end{cases}\]
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= U + \pi + \overbrace{t\cdot x_1}^{\text{Tax income}}\\ &= \big[u(x_1^D,u_2^D)+y\big] + \big[p_1 x_1^D + p_2 x_2^D - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) \big] + t\cdot x_1^D \\ &= \big[u(x_1^D,u_2^D)+ z - x_1^D\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2^D\cdot p_2 \big] \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+\big[p_1 x_1^D + p_2 x_2^D - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) \big] + t\cdot x_1^D \\ &= u(x_1^D,u_2^D) - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) + z\end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1^D} \cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2^D} \cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t}\\ &= u_1\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} + u_2\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t} - c_1\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} - c_2\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t}\\ &= (p_1 + t - p_1) \cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} + (p_2 - p_2)\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t} \\ &= t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\]
    • \(\implies\) sufficient statistic for welfare

Optimal Taxation (Externality)

  • Consumers
    • Utility: \[U = u(x_1,u_2) + y -\underbrace{\gamma x_1}_{\text{Externality}}\]
    • Because consumers are myopic, they will not consider externality when deciding demand!
    • Demand (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}x_1^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_1 &= p_1 + t \\ x_2^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= U + \pi + t\cdot x_1\\ &= \big[u(x_1^D,x_2^D)+y\big] + \big[p_1 x_1^D + p_2 x_2^D - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) \big] \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+ (t-\gamma)\cdot x_1^D \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= (t-\gamma)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\] therefore, optimal tax: \[t^* =\gamma\]

Optimal Taxation (Heterogeneity)

  • \(N\) consumers:
    • Utility: \[U^i = u^i(x_1^i,u_2^i) + y\]
    • Budget: \[\begin{aligned}y &= z^i - x_1^i\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2^i\cdot p_2\\ z^i &\geq (p_1+t)\cdot x_1^i + p_2x_2^i \end{aligned}\]
    • Individual demand: \[\begin{aligned}x_1^i(p_1,p_2,t): && u_1^i &= p_1 + t \\ x_2^i(p_1,p_2,t): && u_2^i &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • Aggregate demand: \[\begin{cases}x_1(p_1,p_2,t) = \sum x_1^i(p_1,p_2,t) \\ x_2(p_1,p_2,t) = \sum x_2^i(p_1,p_2,t) \end{cases}\]
  • Since \(p=c\), firms always make zero profit, so we can ignore the firms
  • Social planner
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= \sum \big[u^i(x_1^i,x_2^i)+ z^i - x_1^i\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2^i\cdot p_2 \big] + t\cdot\sum x_1^i \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1} \cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2} \cdot \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial t}\\ &= \sum (u_1^i - p_1 - t)\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^i}{\partial t} +\sum (u_2^i - p_2)\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^i}{\partial t} + t\cdot\sum \frac{\partial x_1^i}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\]

Optimal Taxation (Internality)

  • Consumers
    • Utility: \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(x_1,u_2) + y \\ &= u(x_1,u_2) + z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • Budget: \[\begin{aligned}y &= z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\\ z &\geq (p_1+t)\cdot x_1 + p_2x_2 \end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Thinks that consumer utility should be: \[V = U-\underbrace{\gamma x_1}_{\text{Internality}}\]
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= U + t\cdot x_1 - \gamma x_1\\ &= \big[u(x_1,x_2)+z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\big] + (t-\gamma)\cdot x_1 \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= (t-\gamma)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\] therefore, optimal tax: \[t^* =\gamma\]

Taxation and Consumer Choise: O’Donoghue & Rabin (2006)

Model

  • Utility: \[U = \underbrace{u(x_1,x_2) + x_3}_{\text{Immediate reward}} - \underbrace{\beta\delta\cdot c x_1}_{\text{Delayed cost}}\]
    • \(x_1\), \(x_2\): goods with concave utility curve
    • \(x_1\): sin good (has delayed health cost)
    • \(x_3\): \(p=1\), no income effect (all money left)
  • Question: how can taxing \(x_1\) affect welfare?
  • Timing
    • \(t=0\): nothing happens
    • \(t=1\): make consumption choices
    • \(t=2\): delayed cost is realised
  • Budget constraint: \(x_3 = z - (p_1 +t) x_1 - p_2 x_2\)
  • Consumer’s problem: \[\begin{aligned}\max && U &= u(x_1,x_2) + x_3 - \beta\delta\cdot c x_1\\ FOC && u_1 &= p_1 + t + \beta\delta\cdot c \equiv \frac{\partial u(x_1,x_2)}{\partial x_1} \\ && u_2 &= p_2 \equiv \frac{\partial u(x_1,x_2)}{\partial x_2}\end{aligned}\]

Welfare effects of Taxation

Identifying Salience Bias

  • Similar to Chetty, Looney, Kroft (2009)
  • Consumer utility: \[\begin{aligned}U=u(x_1, x_2) + z - \theta p_1x_1 - p_2x_2 \end{aligned}\]
    • \(x_1\): resource good (water, energy, etc.)
    • \(p_1\): cost of resource that is hard to perceive
    • FOC: \[\begin{aligned}u_1 &= \theta p_1\\u_2 &= p_2 \end{aligned}\]
    • Comparative statics: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{p_1}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}} <0 \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\theta}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}} <0 \end{aligned}\]
  • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - p_1x_1 - p_2x_2 \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2}\cdot\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial \theta} \\ &= (u_1 - p_1)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} + (\underbrace{u_2-p_2}_{=0})\cdot\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial \theta} \\ &= (\underbrace{\theta p_1 - p_1}_{<0})\cdot\frac{p_1}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}} >0 \end{aligned}\]
    • Increase in \(\theta\implies\) increse in \(W\)

Assumptions

  • (A1): constant second derivatives \[\begin{aligned}H &= u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22} \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\theta}{H} \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{p_1}{H} \end{aligned}\]
  • (A2): with feedback, \(\theta=1\) (perfect nudge)
    • Consumption change: \(\Delta x_1^*\)
    • Salience bias change: \(\Delta\theta = 1-\theta\)
    • Therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\Delta x_1}{\Delta \theta} \\ &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta}\end{aligned}\]

Strategy 1: Compare Consumption w/ or w/o Feedback

  • Aim: eliminate \(\partial\theta\)
  • With feedback
    • \(\theta=1\)
    • With (A1) and (A2), \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta} \\ &= \frac{p_1}{H} \\ &= \frac{\theta}{H}\cdot p_1 \\ &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1 \end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1 &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta} \\ 1-\theta &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1} \end{aligned}\]
    • Intuition: increase in \(\theta\implies\) perceived price increase by \((1-\theta)\cdot p_1\)
    • \(\implies\) same response if price increase w/o feedback
  • Without feedback
    • \(\theta<1\)
    • With (A1) and (A2), let elasticity \(\eta\) be s.t. \[\begin{aligned}\eta &=\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta}\cdot\frac{\theta}{x_1} = \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta}\cdot\frac{\theta}{x_1}\\ &= \frac{p_1}{H}\cdot \frac{\theta}{x_1} \\ &= \frac{\theta}{H}\cdot \frac{p_1}{x_1} \\ &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot\frac{p_1}{x_1} \end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\eta &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta}\cdot\frac{\theta}{x_1}\\ \eta\cdot(1-\theta )\cdot x_1 &= \Delta x_1^*\cdot \theta \\ \eta x_1 - \eta x_1\cdot \theta &= \Delta x_1^*\cdot \theta \\ \theta &= \frac{\eta\cdot x_1}{\Delta x_1^* + \eta x_1} \\ &= \frac{\eta}{\eta + \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{x_1}} \end{aligned}\]

  • Therefore, for all \(k\), \[\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial p_1} = \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} = \frac{1}{H}\]
  • For type \(k\)
    • Welfare effect: \[W^k(1) - W^k(\theta^k) = -p_1 \cdot \Delta x_1^{*,k} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\cdot(1-\theta^k)\]
    • Estimation of \(\theta\): \[1-\theta^k = \frac{\Delta x_1^{*,k}}{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}W^k(1) - W^k(\theta^k) &= -p_1 \cdot (\Delta x_1^*)^k \cdot \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{\Delta x_1^{*,k}}{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1}\\ &= -\frac{1}{2\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}} \cdot (\Delta x_1^{*,k})^2 \end{aligned}\]

    • Ignoring variance \(\implies\) underestimate welfare gain from feedback
    • Non-linearity of welfare gain: high bias \(\implies\) large \(\theta\) gain, large \(\Delta x_1^{*,k}\) change

Habit Stock Model

  • Baseline (standard) model \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(c_t, h_t)+z - p_t c_t\\ h_t &= \alpha\cdot h_t + c_{t-1}\end{aligned}\]
    • Some good is habit-forming if higher habit stock \(\implies\) higher MU of consumption \[u_{ch} = \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial c\partial h}>0\]
    • Good habit: \(u_h>0\)
    • Bad habit: \(u_h<0\)
  • Canonical habit stock model \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(c_t, h_t)- \theta\cdot p_t c_t\\ h_t &= \alpha\cdot h_{t-1} + (1-\alpha)\cdot c_{t-1}\end{aligned}\]
    • \(u_{ch}>0\): habit forming
  • Feedback \(\implies \theta\to 0\implies\) change behaviour \(\implies\) change \(h_t\)

Habit Attention Model

  • Behavioural model \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(c_t) - \theta_t\cdot p c_t \\ \theta_t &= \begin{cases}1 & \text{Feedback} \\ \omega_t & \text{No feedback} \end{cases}\\ \omega_t &= \begin{cases}\alpha\cdot 1 + (1-\alpha)\cdot \omega_{t-1} & \text{Feedback} \\ \beta\cdot \theta + (1-\beta)\cdot \omega_{t-1} & \text{No feedback} \end{cases} \end{aligned}\]
    • \(\alpha<1\): habit build speed
    • \(\beta<1\): return to before state speed
  • Length of intervention phase (feedback) determines the length of persistent

Labour Market

  • Social preferences can change incentives for wage
    • Wage rigidity
    • More unemployment (?)
  • Firm’s profit function \[\pi(L,w) = \underbrace{f\big( e(w)\cdot L \big)}_{\text{Technology}} - w\cdot L\]
    • \(L\): labour
    • \(e(w)\): effort depends on wage, reverse not true

Selfish Workers

  • Worker’s utility \[\begin{aligned}U &= w - c\big(e(w)\big) \\ \frac{\partial U}{\partial e} &= \underbrace{c'(e)}_{>0}\cdot e'(w) \overset{!}{=}0\\ e'(w) &= 0 \\ e(w) &= e^{\min} \text{ for all }w \end{aligned}\]
  • Therefore, firm pays lowest possible wage: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial L} &= f'(e^{\min}\cdot L)\cdot e^{\min} - w \overset{!}{=}0\\ w^* &= f'(e^{\min}\cdot L)\cdot e^{\min}\end{aligned}\]
    • No involuntary unemployment for competitive labour market

Non-Selfish Workers

  • Worker’s utility
    • \(e'(w)>0\), \(e''(w)<0\)
  • Therefore, firm set wage s.t. \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial w} = f'\big( e(w)\cdot L\big)\cdot \frac{\partial e}{\partial w}\cdot L - L &\overset{!}{=}0\\ f'\big( e(w)\cdot L\big)\cdot \frac{\partial e}{\partial w} &= 1 \end{aligned}\] and \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial L} = f'\big( e(w)\cdot L\big)\cdot e - w &\overset{!}{=}0\\ f'\big( e(w)\cdot L\big)\cdot e &= w \\ f'\big( e(w)\cdot L\big) &= \frac{w}{e} \end{aligned}\] therefore, Solow condition: \[\frac{\partial e}{\partial w}\cdot \frac{w}{e} = 1\]

Implications

  • Solow condition
    • Employees earn non-competitive rent
    • Firm does not pay lowest possible wage
    • Higher wage \(\implies\) higher effort
    • \(\implies\) firm can minimise cost per unit of labour
  • Involuntary unemployment
    • Unemployed no long erexert wage pressure, unless it affects \(e(w)\) (?)
    • \(\implies\) systematic unemployment
  • Possible \(e(w)\)
    • Continuous
    • Reference wage: \(e(w, w^R)\)
  • Lab experiment
    • Effort increase on wage level
    • Stronger in long term employments
    • Wage does not converge to competitive level
  • Field experiment

Models of Social Influence

  • Choice: prosocial activity \(a\in\{0,1\}\)
  • Social approval utility: \[u_i = \begin{cases}0 & a=0 \\ b + \theta\cdot p - c_i & a=1\end{cases}\]
    • \(b\): intrinsic benefit
    • \(\theta\cdot p\): impact of social influence
    • \(\theta<1\): perceived social benefit
    • \(p\): fraction of population who choose \(a=1\)
    • \(c_i\): stochastic cost with CDF: \(F(c)\)
  • Social influence increases prosocial activity:
    • Fraction of population who choose \(a=1\): \[\begin{aligned}p &= \Pr(u_i\geq 0)\\ &= \Pr(b + \theta\cdot p\geq c_i)\\ &= F(b + \theta\cdot p)\end{aligned}\] which holds even when \(\theta=0\)
    • Equilibrium: \[p^* = F(b + \theta\cdot p^*)\]
  • When \(c_i\) is uniformly distributed on \([0,1]\), \(b+\theta <1\), \[\begin{aligned}p^* &= F(b + \theta\cdot p^*) \\ &= \frac{b + \theta\cdot p^* - 0}{1-0} \\ &= b + \theta\cdot p^* \\ &= \frac{b}{1-\theta} \end{aligned}\]
  • Social pressure utility: \[u_i = \begin{cases}-\theta\cdot p & a=0 \\ b - c_i & a=1\end{cases}\]
    • Social approval & social pressure model yield same behaviour

Peer Effects

  • Social influence leads to peer effects
    • \(q\): fraction of treatment group
    • \(\gamma\): additional incentive to act
    • Treatment group act if: \[\begin{aligned}u_T &= b + \gamma + \theta\cdot\big( q\cdot p_T + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) - c_i\\ &\geq 0 \\ p_T &= F\Big(b + \gamma + \theta\cdot\big( q\cdot p_T + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) \Big) \end{aligned}\]
    • Control group: \[\begin{aligned}u_C &= b + \theta\cdot\big( q\cdot p_T + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) - c_i\\ &\geq 0 \\ p_C &= F\Big(b + \theta\cdot\big( \underbrace{q\cdot p_T}_{\text{Affected by }\gamma} + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) \Big) \end{aligned}\]
    • Motivational spillover: CG contribution is increased by \(\gamma\) through \(p_T\)
  • When \(c_i\) is uniformly distributed on \([0,1]\),
    • Treatment group: \[\begin{aligned}p_T &= F\Big(b + \gamma + \theta\cdot\big( q\cdot p_T + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) \Big) \\ &= b + \gamma + \theta\cdot\big( q\cdot p_T + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) \\ &= \frac{b + \gamma + \theta(1-q)\cdot p_C}{1-\theta\cdot q}\end{aligned}\]
    • Control group: \[\begin{aligned}p_C &= F\Big(b + \theta\cdot\big( \underbrace{q\cdot p_T}_{\text{Affected by }\gamma} + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big) \Big) \\ &= b + \theta\cdot\big(q\cdot p_T + (1-q)\cdot p_C \big)\\ &= \frac{b + \theta \cdot q\cdot p_T}{1-\theta(1-q)}\end{aligned}\]
    • Therefore, \[\begin{aligned}p_C &= \frac{b + q\cdot \theta\cdot\gamma}{1-\theta}\\ &= \underbrace{\frac{b}{1-\theta}}_{p^* \text{ w/o treatment}} + \underbrace{\frac{q\cdot \theta\cdot\gamma}{1-\theta}}_{\text{Spillover}} \\ p_T &= \frac{b - \theta\gamma + (1+\theta\cdot q)\gamma}{1-\theta}\\ &=\frac{b + q\cdot \theta\cdot\gamma + \gamma(1-\theta)}{1-\theta} \\ &= p_C + \gamma \end{aligned}\]
    • Total effect \[\begin{aligned}TE &= \gamma\cdot q + \frac{q\cdot \theta\cdot\gamma}{1-\theta}\cdot 1\\ &= \frac{(1-\theta)\gamma\cdot q + \theta\cdot\gamma\cdot q}{1-\theta}\\ &= \underbrace{\gamma\cdot q}_{\text{Effect}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{1}{1-\theta}}_{\text{Multiplier}} \end{aligned}\]
  • Policy multiplier: social influence among individuals
    • Since \(\theta<1\), \(\frac{1}{1-\theta}>1\)

Estimating θ

  • Model: \[y_i = x_i'\cdot\beta + \gamma_1\cdot z_i + \gamma_2\cdot \bar{z}_i + \epsilon_i\]
    • \(y_i\in\{0,1\}\): prosocial activity
    • \(z_i = 1(TG)\)
    • \(\bar{z}_i=q\): fraction of TG
  • Estimation \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\gamma_2}{\gamma_1} &= \frac{\theta}{1-\theta}\\ \theta &= \frac{\gamma_2 / \gamma_1}{1 + \gamma_2 / \gamma_1} \end{aligned}\]

Theory: Bénabou & Tirole (2006)

  • Choice: prosocial activity \(a\in\{0,1\}\)
  • Consumption utility \[u^i(a,y) = \begin{cases}0 & a=0 \\ v_a^i + v_y^i\cdot y - c & a=1 \end{cases}\]
    • \(v_a\): value of engaging in \(a\) (altruism)
    • \(v_y\): value of money (greed)
    • \(y\): incentive (rate) to engage in \(a\)
    • \(c\): cost of \(a\)
  • Reputation utility \[\begin{aligned}R(y) &= \overbrace{\gamma_a\cdot E[v_a\ |\ a, y]}^{\text{Social approval}} -\overbrace{\gamma_y \cdot E[v_y\ |\ a,y]}^{\begin{subarray}{1}\text{Bad reputation}\\\text{of being greedy} \end{subarray}} \\ &= \gamma_a\cdot \big( E[v_a\ |\ a=1, y] - E[v_a\ |\ a=0, y] \big) \\ &\phantom{\ggg} - \gamma_y \cdot \big( E[v_y\ |\ a=1, y] - E[v_y\ |\ a=0, y] \big)\end{aligned}\]
    • \(\gamma_a\): weight of appearing altrustic
    • \(\gamma_y\): weight of not appearing greedy
  • Optimal choice \[\begin{aligned}\max_{a} && U^i(a,y) &= a\cdot\big( u^i(a,y)+R(y)\big) \\ && &= a\cdot\big( v_a^i + v_y^i\cdot y - c + R(y) \big) \end{aligned}\] therefore, \(a=1\) if \[v_a^i + v_y^i\cdot y + R(y)\geq c\]
    • When \(y=0\), agents give only for altruism
    • Marginal increase of \(y\) above 0 attracts the most greedy people
      \(\implies\) image-spoiling effect (crowding out effect)
    • As \(y\) increases, incentive effect > image-spoiling effect
