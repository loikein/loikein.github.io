Behavioural Public Policy

Departures from the standard model:

  • Present bias: can not correctly understand future self
  • Salience bias: can only notice the most obvious thing
  • Non-selfish preferences: contribute to public goods

which lead to:

  • Create scope for policy intervention (?)
  • Worsen market failures
  • Reduce market failures

Course overview:

  1. Behavioural theory
  2. Empirical evidence
  3. Quantifying welfare effects of policy

Present Bias

  • Self-control problems \(\implies\) internality

Theory: Intertemporal Consumption

  • Utility discounting: \[U = u(c_t) + \beta \big[\delta u(c_{t+1}) + \delta^2 u(c_{t+2}) + \delta^3 u(c_{t+3}) + \cdots + \delta^T u(c_{t+T})\big]\]
    • Standard model: \(\beta=1\)
    • Present bias: \(\beta<1\)
  • Budget constraint (can borrow): \[c_t + c_{t+1} + \dots + c_{t+T} \leq y_t + y_{t+1} + \dots + y_{t+T}\]
    • Total available assets: \[\begin{aligned}a_t &= y_t + y_{t+1} + \dots + y_{t+T} \\ a_{t+1} &= a_t - c_t\end{aligned}\]
  • Bellman equation: for all \(s = t, t+1,\dots, t+T\) \[\begin{aligned}V_t(a_t) &= \max_{c_t} \ u(c_t) + \delta\cdot V_{t+1}(a_t - c_t) \\ &= \max_{c_t} \ u(c_t) + \delta\cdot \underbrace{ u\big(c_{t+1}(a_t - c_t)\big) +\delta\cdot V_{t+2}\big(a_t - c_t - c_{t+1}(a_t - c_t)\big)}_{V_{t+1}(a_t - c_t)}\end{aligned}\] Therefore, FOC: \[\begin{aligned}u'(c_s) &= \delta \cdot V'_{s+1}(a_s - c_s) \\ &= \delta \cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a} \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+\delta^2\cdot V'_{s+2}\big(a_t - c_t - c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \Big(1-\frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a}\Big)\\ u'(c_{s+1}) &= \delta\cdot V'_{s+2}(a_{s+1} - c_{s+1}) \\ u'(c_s) &= \delta\cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a} \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+ \delta\cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \cdot \big(1 -\frac{\partial c_{s+1}}{\partial a}\big)\\ &= \delta\cdot u'\big(c_{s+1}(a_s - c_s)\big) \end{aligned}\] \(\implies\) Euler equation

Agents

  • TC
    • \(\beta=1\)
    • Set consumption s.t. \[u'(c_t^{TC}) = \delta\cdot u'(c_{t+1}^{TC})\]
    • (In standard model:) \[u'(c_t^{TC}) = \frac{p_t}{p_{t+1}}\cdot u'(c_{t+1}^{TC})\]
  • Naif
    • \(\beta <1\), but think themselves will behave like TC in the future \(\implies\) over-consumption in this period
    • Set consumption s.t. \[u'(c_t^{N}) = \beta\delta\cdot u'(c_{t+1}^{N})\]
  • Sophisticate
    • \(\beta <1\), and they know it
    • Set consumption s.t. \[u'(c_t^S) = u'(c_{t+1}^S)\cdot\Big(\beta\delta\cdot\frac{\delta c_{t+1}^S}{\delta a}+\delta\big(1-\frac{\delta c_{t+1}^S}{\delta a}\big)\Big)\]
    • If \(\frac{\delta c_{t+1}^S}{\delta a}\approx 1\), no motive to save
    • If \(\frac{\delta c_{t+1}^S}{\delta a}\ll 1\), save & more patient
  • Naif vs. Sophisticate
    • Naif’s false optimism help to reduce current consumption
    • Sophisticate’s correct self assessment help reduce current consumption

Iso-Elastic Utility

  • Utility function: \[u(c) = \frac{1}{1-\sigma}\cdot c^{1-\sigma}\]
  • TC’s consumption: \[\Delta \log c_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot\Delta\log p_t + \frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot\log\delta\] Therefore, monthly consumption: \[\Delta \log c_{t+30} - \log c_t = -\frac{1}{\sigma}\cdot(\log p_{t+30} -\log p_t) + 30\cdot\frac{1}{\sigma}\] if price does not change: \[\Delta \log c_{t+30} - \log c_t \approx 30\cdot\frac{1}{\sigma}<0\]

Work Allocation: (2015 & 2016)

  • Augenblick, Niederle, Sprenger (2015)
  • Andreoni, Callen, Khan, Jaffar, Sprenger (2016)

Lab Experiment

  • Procrastination & present bias in work
  • Tedious work for two sessions
    • \(t=0\): initial allocation: \(e_1\) and \(e_2\)
    • \(t=1\): re-allocation; randomly pick between the allocations; session 1
    • \(t=2\): session 2
  • Notations
    • \(R\): interest rate
    • \(B\): basic income
    • \(e_1\) and \(e_2\): effort costs
    • \(e_1 + R e_2 = 50\): budget constraint
  • \(t=0\) \[\begin{aligned}\max_{e_1^0,e_2^0} && U_0 &= \beta\delta^2 B - \beta (\delta c(e_1) + \delta^2 c(e_2))\\ FOC && c'(e_1) &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2)\end{aligned}\]
    • \(\frac{\delta}{R}=1\implies e_1=e_2\)
    • \(\frac{\delta}{R}<1\implies e_1<e_2\)
    • \(\frac{\delta}{R}>1\implies e_1>e_2\)
  • \(t=1\) \[\begin{aligned}\max_{e_1^1,e_2^1} && U_1 &= \beta\delta B - (c(e_1) + \beta\delta c(e_2))\\ FOC && c'(e_1) &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2) < \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2)\end{aligned}\]
    • \(\implies e_1^1<e_1^0\), \(e_2^1>e_2^0\)
    • 5% - 10% procrastination
    • Is that a lot?

Calibration (Specifying the Model by Guessing)

  • Assume \(c(e)=0.5\cdot c\cdot e^2\)
    • \(c'(e) = ce\)
  • When \(t=0\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^0) &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^0)\\ ce_1^0 &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot ce_2^0 \\ \implies \delta &= R\cdot\frac{e_1^0}{e_2^0}\end{aligned}\]
  • When \(t=1\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^1) &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^1) \\ ce_1^1 &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot ce_2^1 \\ \implies \beta\delta &= R\cdot\frac{e_1^1}{e_2^1}\end{aligned}\]
  • Therefore, present bias: \[\beta = \frac{e_1^1\ /\ e_2^1}{e_1^0\ /\ e_2^0}\]

Structural Estimation (Better Guessing with Generalisation)

  • Assume \(c(e)=\frac{1}{\gamma}\cdot c\cdot e^{\gamma}\)
    • \(\gamma\geq 1\)
  • When \(t=0\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^0) &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^0)\\ c(e_1^0)^{\gamma -1} &= \frac{\delta}{R}\cdot c(e_2^0)^{\gamma -1} \end{aligned}\] take log of both sides: \[\begin{aligned}(\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_1^0 &= \log \delta - \log R + (\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_2^0 \\ \log e_2^0 - \log e_1^0 &= \frac{1}{\gamma -1}(- \log \delta + \log R) \end{aligned}\]
  • When \(t=1\), FOC: \[\begin{aligned}c'(e_1^1) &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c'(e_2^1) \\ c(e_1^1)^{\gamma -1} &= \frac{\beta\delta}{R}\cdot c(e_2^1)^{\gamma -1} \end{aligned}\] take log of both sides: \[\begin{aligned}(\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_1^1 &= \log \beta + \log \delta - \log R + (\gamma -1)\cdot\log e_2^1 \\ \log e_2^1 - \log e_1^1 &= \frac{1}{\gamma -1}(- \log \beta - \log \delta + \log R )\end{aligned}\]
  • \(\implies\) Econometrics model: \[\log e_{2ik}^t - \log e_{1ik}^t = \underbrace{\phi_0}_{\frac{-\log \delta}{\gamma -1}} +\underbrace{\phi_1}_{\frac{-\log \beta}{\gamma -1}}\cdot 1(t=1) +\underbrace{\phi_2}_{\frac{1}{\gamma -1}}\cdot \log R_k + \epsilon_{ikt}\]
    • \(k\): different values of \(R\)
    • \(\phi_2\): elasticity of effort wrt \(R\)
    • \(\delta = \exp\big(-\frac{\phi_0}{\phi_2}\big)\)
    • \(\beta = \exp\big(-\frac{\phi_1}{\phi_2}\big)\)

Field Experiment

  • Between-subject design
    • Advance choice group: \(e_1^0\), \(e_2^0\)
    • Immediate choice group: \(e_1^1\), \(e_2^1\)
  • Budget constraint: \(e_1 + R e_2 \geq 300\)
    • If actual work reaches \(e\), receive bonus

Round 1

  • For each value of \(R\), subjects have to choose \(e\)’s

Round 2 (same group of subjects)

  • Induce equalised effort over periods
    • Tailored group: set \(R\) s.t. \(R_i=\beta_i \cdot \delta_i\)
    • Untailored group: randomly assign \(R\)

Food Choice: Sadoff, Samek, Sprenger (2014 & 2019)

  • 2014
  • 2019

Purpose:

  • Present-bias in food choice
  • Commitment tool
    • Self-control problems \(\implies\) unhealthy diet & high BMI
  • Strength of replication

Experiment

  • \(t=0\): pick item 1
  • \(t=1\): get item 1, pick 2
  • \(t=2\): get 2, pick 3
    • No commitment group: change item 2
  • \(t=3\): get 3
    • No commitment group: change item 3
  • Result: more consistent subjects tend to choose commitment
    • \(\implies\) stronger the bias, less aware of it

Food Stamp: Shapiro (2005)

Experiment

  • Measure actual (daily) consumption, not monetary expenditure
  • Caloric intakes decreases by 0.45% each day after receiving food stamp

Explaining Using Standard Model

  • Assume \(\sigma=1\), \(\implies\delta=\exp(-0.0045)\approx 0.995\),
    which makes yearly discount factor \(\delta^{365}=0.16\) (too low!)
  • Assume \(\delta^{365}=0.95\), therefore elasticity: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\log\delta}{\sigma} &= -0.0045 \\ \frac{1}{\sigma} &= \frac{-0.0045}{\log 0.95^{1/365}} \\ &= 32.02\end{aligned}\] \(\implies\) 1% change in price cause 32% decrease in consumption (too large!)

Behavioural Model

  • The value of calorie for each day is different:
    • Running out of money
    • Have to skip a meal because running out of money
  • Assume sophisticate, \(\beta=0.96\)
  • Assume naif \(\beta\) is even closer to 1

Retirement: Haider & Stephens (2007)

  • In standard model…
    • Assume consumption is smooth
    • Unaffected by retirement (time)
    • But if borrowing option drops with retirement, income falls so consumption must change
  • Problems
    • Retirements can be unexpected
    • People cannot predict wage perfectly
  • \(\implies\) present-biased preferences

Estimating Consumption

  • Model 1: \[\Delta\log c_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot 1(\text{retired})_{it} + \beta_2' x_{it} + \epsilon_{it}\]
    • \(\Delta\log c_{it}\): percentage change by household \(i\) in year \(t\)
    • \(x_{it}\): control variables
    • \(H_0\): \(\beta_1=0\) (retirement does not affect consumption)
    • Result: consumption drop by about 10%
  • Model 2 with instrumental variable:
    • Instrument: expected retirement year
    • Result: consumption drop by about 10%

Financial Desicion: Chetty et al (2014)

  • Financial decisions is affected by present bias
    • Immediate costs, future benefits
    • Want to pospone to \(t+1\)
    • Procrastination leads to huge losses

Pension system in Denmark

  • Social security from state
  • Employer-linked pension scheme
  • Individual pension scheme

Experiment

  • Changes in employer contribution
    • 1% change in employer contribution \(\implies\) 0.8% change in saving rate
    • When employer contribution increases (switch job, etc.), personal savings should drop
    • But no
  • Changes in subsidy (similar)

Increase Employee Saving: Thaler & Benartzi (2004)

  • Good financial decision should be free from…
    • Present bias: choose savings rate independent from \(\beta\)
    • Procrastination: make timely decisions
    • Loss aversion: increase saving \(\implies\) decrease now consumption

“Save More Tomorrow”

  • Present bias: decide savings rate 3 month ahead
  • Procrastination: save by default, can opt out
  • Loss aversion: savings come from pay rise

Firm 1

  • Financial advisor: recommend higher savings rate
    • If accept: implement
    • If reject: SMT programme (increase savings rate by 3 %points every year, based on pay rise)
  • Result: people who entered SMT programme ended up with higher savings rate
  • Problems: self-selection

Firm 2

  • SMT programme: set increase rate \(x\%\), stop if \(x\geq 10\)
  • Difference-in-difference: only two divisions have SMT, other divisions are control groups
  • Result: smaller increase than firm 1
    • No pay rise guaranteed
    • Clearer control groups

Power of Default: Blumenstock, Callen, Ghani (2018)

  • Problems of previous studies: not truly exogenous
    • Self-selection
    • Endogenous timings

Field Experiment

  • Banking system in Afghanistan
    • No formal banks
    • Shadow banks: mobile phone accounts
  • Mobile firm whose employees receive payment through phone account
    • Set up new account for pension savings
    • Employees can withdraw early, but will lose firm rate
  • Randomise contribution rate
    • Employee: 0% or 5% (can call to change)
    • Employer: 0%, 25% or 50%

Results

  • Default settings have huge impact on choices
    • Employees barely change the rates
    • Those who did happened early on
    • Default affect attitudes towards saving

Theory

    • Produce first-order gain for biased agents
    • Produce loss for rational agents

Paternalistic paternalism:

  • Self-control problems cannot be fixed by libertarian intervention

  • Examples

Sufficient Statistic Welfare Analysis

  • Harberger’s Triangle (Harberger, 1962): \[\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} = t\cdot\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\]
    • Treat internality as externality
    • Sufficient statistics
    • Heterogeneous (mixed rational & behavioural) agents
  • Estimate welfare costs of taxation w/o full details of utility & production functions

Optimal Tadxation (Benchmark)

  • Price-taking consumers
    • Utility: \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(x_1,u_2) + y \\ &= u(x_1,u_2) + z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • Budget: \[\begin{aligned}y &= z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\\ z &\geq (p_1+t)\cdot x_1 + p_2x_2 \end{aligned}\]
    • Demand (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}x_1^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_1 &= p_1 + t \\ x_2^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • \(y\): \(p=1\), no income effect (all money left)
    • \(t\): tax rate (assume that consumers are nominal tax payer)
  • Price-taking firms
    • Profit function: \[\pi = p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 - c(x_1, x_2)\]
    • Supply (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}x_1^S(p_1,p_2): && c_1 &= p_1 \\ x_2^S(p_1,p_2): && c_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Market clearance: \[\begin{cases}x_1^D = x_1^S \\ x_2^D = x_2^S \end{cases}\]
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= U + \pi + \overbrace{t\cdot x_1}^{\text{Tax income}}\\ &= \big[u(x_1^D,u_2^D)+y\big] + \big[p_1 x_1^D + p_2 x_2^D - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) \big] + t\cdot x_1^D \\ &= \big[u(x_1^D,u_2^D)+ z - x_1^D\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2^D\cdot p_2 \big] \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+\big[p_1 x_1^D + p_2 x_2^D - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) \big] + t\cdot x_1^D \\ &= u(x_1^D,u_2^D) - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) + z\end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1^D} \cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2^D} \cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t}\\ &= u_1\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} + u_2\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t} - c_1\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} - c_2\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t}\\ &= (p_1 + t - p_1) \cdot \frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} + (p_2 - p_2)\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^D}{\partial t} \\ &= t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\]
    • \(\implies\) sufficient statistic for welfare

Optimal Tadxation (Externality)

  • Consumers
    • Utility: \[U = u(x_1,u_2) + y -\underbrace{\gamma x_1}_{\text{Externality}}\]
    • Because consumers are myopic, they will not consider externality when deciding demand!
    • Demand (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}x_1^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_1 &= p_1 + t \\ x_2^D(p_1,p_2,t): && u_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= U + \pi + t\cdot x_1\\ &= \big[u(x_1^D,x_2^D)+y\big] + \big[p_1 x_1^D + p_2 x_2^D - c(x_1^D, x_2^D) \big] \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+ (t-\gamma)\cdot x_1^D \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= (t-\gamma)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^D}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\] therefore, optimal tax: \[t^* =\gamma\]

Optimal Tadxation (Heterogeneity)

  • \(N\) consumers:
    • Utility: \[U^i = u^i(x_1^i,u_2^i) + y\]
    • Budget: \[\begin{aligned}y &= z^i - x_1^i\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2^i\cdot p_2\\ z^i &\geq (p_1+t)\cdot x_1^i + p_2x_2^i \end{aligned}\]
    • Individual demand: \[\begin{aligned}x_1^i(p_1,p_2,t): && u_1^i &= p_1 + t \\ x_2^i(p_1,p_2,t): && u_2^i &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • Aggregate demand: \[\begin{cases}x_1(p_1,p_2,t) = \sum x_1^i(p_1,p_2,t) \\ x_2(p_1,p_2,t) = \sum x_2^i(p_1,p_2,t) \end{cases}\]
  • Since \(p=c\), firms always make zero profit, so we can ignore the firms
  • Social planner
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= \sum \big[u^i(x_1^i,x_2^i)+ z^i - x_1^i\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2^i\cdot p_2 \big] + t\cdot\sum x_1^i \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1} \cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2} \cdot \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial t}\\ &= \sum (u_1^i - p_1 - t)\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^i}{\partial t} +\sum (u_2^i - p_2)\cdot \frac{\partial x_2^i}{\partial t} + t\cdot\sum \frac{\partial x_1^i}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\]

Optimal Tadxation (Internality)

  • Consumers
    • Utility: \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(x_1,u_2) + y \\ &= u(x_1,u_2) + z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\end{aligned}\]
    • Budget: \[\begin{aligned}y &= z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\\ z &\geq (p_1+t)\cdot x_1 + p_2x_2 \end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Thinks that consumer utility should be: \[V = U-\underbrace{\gamma x_1}_{\text{Internality}}\]
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= U + t\cdot x_1 - \gamma x_1\\ &= \big[u(x_1,x_2)+z - x_1\cdot (p_1+t) - x_2\cdot p_2\big] + (t-\gamma)\cdot x_1 \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= (t-\gamma)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\] therefore, optimal tax: \[t^* =\gamma\]

Taxation and Consumer Choise: O’Donoghue & Rabin (2006)

Model

  • Utility: \[U = \underbrace{u(x_1,x_2) + x_3}_{\text{Immediate reward}} - \underbrace{\beta\delta\cdot c x_1}_{\text{Delayed cost}}\]
    • \(x_1\), \(x_2\): goods with concave utility curve
    • \(x_1\): sin good (has delayed health cost)
    • \(x_3\): \(p=1\), no income effect (all money left)
  • Question: how can taxing \(x_1\) affect welfare?
  • Timing
    • \(t=0\): nothing happens
    • \(t=1\): make consumption choices
    • \(t=2\): delayed cost is realised
  • Budget constraint: \(x_3 = z - (p_1 +t) x_1 - p_2 x_2\)
  • Consumer’s problem: \[\begin{aligned}\max && U &= u(x_1,x_2) + x_3 - \beta\delta\cdot c x_1\\ FOC && u_1 &= p_1 + t + \beta\delta\cdot c \equiv \frac{\partial u(x_1,x_2)}{\partial x_1} \\ && u_2 &= p_2 \equiv \frac{\partial u(x_1,x_2)}{\partial x_2}\end{aligned}\]

Welfare effects of Taxation

  • Social planner: internality case where \(\gamma=\delta(1-\beta) c\) \[\begin{aligned}W &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - (p_1 + t) x_1 - p_2x_2 - \underbrace{\beta\delta\cdot cx_1} +\,tx_1 - \underbrace{\overbrace{\delta(1-\beta) c}^{\text{Internality}} x_1}\\ &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - (p_1 + t) x_1 - p_2x_2 - \delta\cdot c x_1 + tx_1 \end{aligned}\] let \[u_i = \frac{\partial u(x_1,x_2)}{\partial u_i}\] we have: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1} \cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2} \cdot \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial t}\\ &= u_1\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}+u_2\cdot \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial t} -(p_1+t)\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} - x_1\\ &\phantom{\ggg}- p_2\cdot \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial t}-\delta c\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} +x_1 +t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\\ &= \big[u_1 - (p_1+t) - \delta c + t\big]\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} +(\underbrace{u_2 - p_2}_{=0}) \cdot\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial t} \\ &= (\underbrace{p_1 + t + \beta\delta c}_{u_1} - p_1 - t - \delta c + t)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} \\ &= \big[\delta c\cdot(\beta -1) + t\big]\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\]
    • If \(\beta=1\), Harberger’s Triangle: \(\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} = t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\)
    • If \(\beta<1\), internality
  • Let \(\gamma=\delta(1-\beta)c\), \(\beta<1\), \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \big[t - \overbrace{\delta c\cdot(1-\beta)}^{>0}\big]\cdot\overbrace{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}}^{<0}\\ &=(t-\gamma)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} \overset{!}{=} 0 \\ t^* &= \delta c\cdot(1-\beta)\end{aligned}\]
    • \(t^*\): optimal tax rate on \(x_1\)
    • \(\implies\) makes consumer behave as if \(\beta=1\)

Heterogeneity

  • \(K\) types of consumers: \(1=\beta^1>\beta^2\geq \beta^3\dots\geq \beta^K\)
    • Frequency: \(\phi^k\)
    • Average bias: \(E[\beta]=\sum_{k=1}^K \phi^k\cdot \beta^k\)
    • Can only set one \(t\) rate!
  • Welfare of group \(k\): \[\begin{aligned}W^k &= u(x_1^k,x_2^k) + z - (p_1 + t) x_1^k - p_2x_2 - \delta\cdot c x_1 + tx_1 \\ \frac{\partial W^k}{\partial t} &= \big[\underbrace{\delta c\cdot(\beta^k -1)}_{-\gamma^k} + t\big]\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial t} \\ &= (t-\gamma^k)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\]
  • Aggregate demand: \[\begin{aligned}x_1(p_1, p_2, t) &= \sum \phi^k\cdot x_1^k(p_1, p_2, t) \\ \frac{\partial x_1(p_1, p_2, t)}{\partial t} &= \sum \phi^k\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^k(p_1, p_2, t)}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\]
  • Aggregate welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W(t) &= \sum \phi^k\cdot W^k(t) \\ \frac{\partial W(t)}{\partial t} &= \sum \phi^k\cdot \frac{\partial W^k(t)}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\]
  • Aggregate welfare cost of tax: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \sum \phi^k\cdot(t-\gamma^k)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial t} \\ &= t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} - \sum \phi^k\gamma^k \cdot\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial t} \overset{!}{=} 0\\ t^* &= \underbrace{\frac{1}{\partial x_1 / \partial t}}_{\text{Aggregate demand sensitivity}} \cdot\sum \Big[\underbrace{\phi^k\gamma^k}_{\text{Weight} \cdot \text{internality}} \cdot\underbrace{\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial t}}_{\text{Demand response to tax}}\Big] \\ &=\sum\phi^k\gamma^k\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^k / \partial t}{\partial x_1 / \partial t} \end{aligned}\]
    • When \(u_1\) is convex, bias gives sharp derivative, \(t^*> E[\gamma]\)
    • When \(u_1\) is concave, bias gives weaker derivative, \(t^*< E[\gamma]\)
  • Intuition:
    • At \(t=0\), non-TC’s agree with the tax
    • Since TC consume less \(x_1\), they benefit from the tax

Taxation & Happiness: Gruber & Mullainathan (2005)

Models

  • Base model: \[y_{i,j,t} = \alpha_j + \delta_t + \gamma_1 \cdot tax_{jt} + x_{it}'\cdot \beta + \epsilon_{it}\]
    • \(y\): subject wellbeing for consumer \(i\), state \(j\), time \(t\)
    • \(\alpha_j\): fixed effect of \(j\)
    • \(\delta_t\): fixed effect of \(t\)
    • \(\gamma_1\): coefficient for tax
    • \(tax_{jt}\): tax on cigarette in state \(j\), time \(t\)
    • \(x_{it}\): other control variables
  • Probit model: \[\widehat{SM}_{it} = \Pr (i\text{ is a smoker in time }t) = F(x_{it}' \cdot \phi)\]
  • Interaction model: \[\begin{aligned}y_{i,j,t} = &\alpha_j+\delta_t+\gamma_1\cdot tax_{jt}\\ &+\gamma_2\cdot\widehat{SM}_{it}+\gamma_3\cdot\widehat{SM}_{it}\times tax_{jt}+x_{it}'\cdot\beta+\epsilon_{it} \end{aligned}\]
    • \(\gamma_1\): impact of tax, assume \(\Pr(\text{smoking})=0\)
    • \(\gamma_3\): difference of impact of tax, assume \(\Pr(\text{smoking})=1\)

Interpretation

  • Tax and smoking are not independent
    • Former smokers may quit \(\implies\) benefit from tax
  • Result
    • Cigarette tax increase happiness among potential smokers
    • Other tax does not make smokers happy
    • \(\implies\) smoking is affected by self-control problems

Salience Bias

Salience & Taxation: Chetty, Looney, Kroft (2009)

Model

  • Price labels in shops does not include sales tax
    • \(p_1\): label price
    • \(t=0.07p_1\): sales tax
  • Consumers
    • Consumers make decisions as if they only understand a fraction of \(t\)
    • Utility: \[U = u(x_1,x_2) + \underbrace{z - (p_1+\theta t) x_1 - p_2x_2}_{y}\]
    • \(\theta<1\): salience bias
    • Demand (FOC): \[\begin{aligned}u_1 &= p_1 + \theta t \\ u_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
  • Social planner
    • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned} W &= U + t x_1 - \overbrace{(1-\theta)tx_1}^{\text{Add back uncounted tax}} \\ &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - (p_1+\theta t) x_1 - p_2x_2 + t x_1\\ &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - p_1x_1 - p_2x_2 \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \theta t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\]
  • \(\implies\) salience bias lower cost of taxation!
    • Salience bias makes demand less elastic wrt tax
    • Reduces distortion by taxation

Strategy 1: Compare Consumption w/ or w/o Tax

  • Increase \(p_1\) and lower \(t\) by the same amount, \(\partial p_1=\partial t\)
    \(\implies x(t) = x^*(t=0)\)
  • Demand: \[\begin{aligned}u_1^* &= u_1 - \theta t + t \\ u_2^* &= u_2\end{aligned}\]
  • To derive \(\epsilon=\frac{\partial x^*}{dp_1}\), \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial u_1^*}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\partial u_1^*}{\partial t} = 1-\theta \\ \frac{\partial u_2^*}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\partial u_2^*}{\partial t} =0\end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial u_1^*}\cdot\frac{\partial u_1^*}{\partial p_1} +\frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial u_2^*}\cdot\frac{\partial u_2^*}{\partial p_1}\\ &= (1-\theta)\frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial u_1^*} + 0\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial u_2^*} \\ &= (1-\theta)\frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial u_1^*}\end{aligned}\]
  • On the other hand, if \(t\) does not exist from the first place, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1}\cdot\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial p_1} +\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_2}\cdot\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial p_1}\\ &= 1\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1}+0\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_2}\\ &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1}\end{aligned}\] where (?) \[\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1} = \frac{1}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}}\]
  • Salience bias can be estimated as: \[\frac{\partial x_1^*/\partial p_1}{\partial x_1/\partial p_1}=1-\theta\]

Experiment:

  • Treatment stores: tag with tax-inclusive prices
  • Control groups: observe same goods w/o change in tags
    • Group 1: same street
    • Group 2: nearby cities
    • Group 3: previous month of treatment stores
  • Result: demand decreased by 2.2 units of goods per category/week
  • Estimation: \[\begin{aligned}1-\theta &= \frac{x_1^* - x_1}{dx_1^*/d(p_1^*)+2\cdot(dx_1^*/dp_1^*)^2 \cdot (dx_1^*/dp_1)}\\ &=0.81 \\ \theta &\approx 0.2\end{aligned}\]

Strategy 2: Use Different Tax Rates

  • Demand: \[\begin{aligned}u_1 &= p_1 + \theta t \\ u_2 &= p_2\end{aligned}\]
  • For tax, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1}\cdot\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial t} +\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_2}\cdot\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial t}\\ &= \theta\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1}+0\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_2}\\ &= \theta\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial u_1}\end{aligned}\]
  • Salience bias can be estimated as: \[\frac{\partial x_1/\partial t}{\partial x_1/\partial p_1}=\theta\]

Experiment:

  • Alcohol tag price includes tax
  • Estimation: \[\begin{aligned}\theta &= \frac{\partial x_1/\partial t}{\partial x_1/\partial p_1}\\ &=\frac{–0.2}{-0.88} \approx 0.22\end{aligned}\]

Heterogeneity: Taubinsky & Rees-Jones (2018)

  • Consider Harberger’s Triangle with salience bias: \[\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} = \theta t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\] plug in \(\theta=\frac{\partial x_1/\partial t}{\partial x_1/\partial p_1}\) \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \theta t\cdot \theta\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\\ &= \theta^2 t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} \end{aligned}\]
  • Consumer
    • Types of bias level: \(1 = \theta^1 >\theta^2 > \dots > \theta^K\)
    • Type \(k\)’s welfare cost of tax: \[\frac{\partial W^k}{\partial t} = (\theta^k)^2 t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\]
  • Total welfare cost of tax: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} &= \sum \phi^k\cdot \frac{\partial W^k}{\partial t}\\ &= \sum \Big[ \phi^k (\theta^k)^2 t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\Big] \\ &= t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot \sum \phi^k (\theta^k)^2 \\ &= t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot E[\theta^2] \\ &= t\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot \big( E^2[\theta] + Var(\theta) \big)\end{aligned}\]
    • Must consider variance of bias!
  • Non-linearity of welfare cost
    • Level effect: low salience bias \(\implies\) low consumption
    • Responsiveness: low salience bias \(\implies\) strong reaction to tax
  • Source of welfare cost
    • \(E^2[\theta]\): average distortion, disappears if \(x_1\) is inelastic
    • \(Var(\theta)\): mis-allocation of consumption, does not disappear

Setup

  • Utility of \(x_1\): \[u_i = m + \begin{cases} v_i - \tilde{q} & x_1 = 1\\ 0 & x_1 = 0\end{cases}\]
    • \(p\): pre-tax price
    • \(\tilde{q}=p(1+\theta\tau)\): perceived after-tax price

  • Ask for WTP twice
    1. Knowing that have to pay tax later, what’s the maximum tag price? \[\begin{aligned}p_i^N (1+\theta\tau) &= v_i \\ p_i^N &= \frac{v_i}{1+\theta\tau} \end{aligned}\]
    2. Knowing that no tax, what’s the maximum tag price? \[p_i^Z = v_i \]
  • Therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{p_i^Z}{p_i^N} &= 1+\theta\tau \\ \Delta \ln p_i &= \ln p_i^Z - \ln p_i^N \\ &= \ln (1+\theta\tau) \\ &\approx \theta\tau \\ \theta &\approx \frac{\Delta \ln p_i}{\tau} \end{aligned}\]

Identifying Salience Bias

  • Similar to Chetty, Looney, Kroft (2009)
  • Consumer utility: \[\begin{aligned}U=u(x_1, x_2) + z - \theta p_1x_1 - p_2x_2 \end{aligned}\]
    • \(x_1\): resource good (water, energy, etc.)
    • \(p_1\): cost of resource that is hard to perceive
    • FOC: \[\begin{aligned}u_1 &= \theta p_1\\u_2 &= p_2 \end{aligned}\]
    • Comparative statics: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{p_1}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}} <0 \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\theta}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}} <0 \end{aligned}\]
  • Social welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W &= u(x_1,x_2) + z - p_1x_1 - p_2x_2 \\ \frac{\partial W}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial x_1}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} +\frac{\partial W}{\partial x_2}\cdot\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial \theta} \\ &= (u_1 - p_1)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} + (\underbrace{u_2-p_2}_{=0})\cdot\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial \theta} \\ &= (\underbrace{\theta p_1 - p_1}_{<0})\cdot\frac{p_1}{u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22}} >0 \end{aligned}\]
    • Increase in \(\theta\implies\) increse in \(W\)

Assumptions

  • (A1): constant second derivatives \[\begin{aligned}H &= u_{11} - u_{12}^2 / u_{22} \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} &= \frac{\theta}{H} \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{p_1}{H} \end{aligned}\]
  • (A2): with feedback, \(\theta=1\) (perfect nudge)
    • Consumption change: \(\Delta x_1^*\)
    • Salience bias change: \(\Delta\theta = 1-\theta\)
    • Therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\Delta x_1}{\Delta \theta} \\ &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta}\end{aligned}\]

Strategy 1: Compare Consumption w/ or w/o Feedback

  • Aim: eliminate \(\partial\theta\)
  • With feedback
    • \(\theta=1\)
    • With (A1) and (A2), \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta} \\ &= \frac{p_1}{H} \\ &= \frac{\theta}{H}\cdot p_1 \\ &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1 \end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1 &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta} \\ 1-\theta &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1} \end{aligned}\]
    • Intuition: increase in \(\theta\implies\) perceived price increase by \((1-\theta)\cdot p_1\)
    • \(\implies\) same response if price increase w/o feedback
  • Without feedback
    • \(\theta<1\)
    • With (A1) and (A2), let \(\eta\) be s.t. \[\begin{aligned}\eta &=\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta}\cdot\frac{\theta}{x_1} = \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta}\cdot\frac{\theta}{x_1}\\ &= \frac{p_1}{H}\cdot \frac{\theta}{x_1} \\ &= \frac{\theta}{H}\cdot \frac{p_1}{x_1} \\ &= \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot\frac{p_1}{x_1} \end{aligned}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}\eta &= \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\theta}\cdot\frac{\theta}{x_1}\\ \eta\cdot(1-\theta )\cdot x_1 &= \Delta x_1^*\cdot \theta \\ \eta x_1 - \eta x_1\cdot \theta &= \Delta x_1^*\cdot \theta \\ \theta &= \frac{\eta\cdot x_1}{\Delta x_1^* + \eta x_1} \\ &= \frac{\eta}{\eta + \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{x_1}} \end{aligned}\]

Strategy 2: Measure Price Response w/ or w/o Feedback

  • Price response with feedback: \[\frac{\partial x_1(p_1,p_2,\theta=1)}{\partial p_1} = \frac{1}{H}\]
  • Price response w/o feedback: \[\frac{\partial x_1(p_1,p_2,\theta)}{\partial p_1} = \frac{\theta}{H}\]
  • Therefore, \(\theta\) is given by \[\theta = \frac{\partial x_1(p_1,p_2,\theta)/\partial p_1}{\partial x_1(p_1,p_2,\theta=1)/\partial p_1}\]

Welfare Effect of Debiasing

  • Compare welfare: \[\begin{aligned}W(\theta=1) - W(\theta) &= \int_{\theta}^1 p_1(s-1)\frac{p_1}{H}\, ds \\ &= -p_1 \cdot \int_{\theta}^1 (1-s)\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta}\, ds \\ &= -p_1 \cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta}\cdot\int_{\theta}^1 (1-s)\, ds \\ W(\theta=1) - W(\theta=\hat{\theta}) &= -p_1 \cdot \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\hat{\theta}}\cdot \int_{\hat{\theta}}^1 (1-s)\, ds \\ &= -p_1 \cdot \frac{\Delta x_1^*}{1-\hat{\theta}}\cdot \frac{1 - 2\hat{\theta} + \hat{\theta}^2}{2}\\ &= \underbrace{-p_1 \cdot \Delta x_1^*}_{\text{Change in expenditure}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\cdot(1-\hat{\theta})}_{\text{Change in quantity (?)}}\end{aligned}\]

Heterogeneity

  • \(K\) types of consumers: \(1=\theta^1 > \theta^2 >\dots>\theta^K\)
  • No heterogeneity in second derivatives
  • Therefore, for all \(k\), \[\frac{\partial x_1^k}{\partial p_1} = \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1} = \frac{1}{H}\]
  • For type \(k\)
    • Welfare effect: \[W^k(1) - W^k(\theta^k) = -p_1 \cdot \Delta x_1^{*,k} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\cdot(1-\theta^k)\]
    • Estimation of \(\theta\): \[1-\theta^k = \frac{\Delta x_1^{*,k}}{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}W^k(1) - W^k(\theta^k) &= -p_1 \cdot (\Delta x_1^*)^k \cdot \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{\Delta x_1^{*,k}}{\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\cdot p_1}\\ &= -\frac{1}{2\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}} \cdot (\Delta x_1^{*,k})^2 \end{aligned}\]
  • Aggregate welfare effects: \[\begin{aligned}W(1) - W(\theta) &= \sum \phi^k\cdot\big(W^k(1) - W^k(\theta^k) \big) \\ &= -\frac{1}{2\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}} \cdot \sum \phi^k \cdot (\Delta x_1^{*,k})^2 \\ &= -\frac{1}{2\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}} \cdot E\big[ (\Delta x_1^{*,k})^2 \big] \\ &= -\frac{1}{2\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}} \cdot\big( E^2[\Delta x_1^{*,k}] + Var(\Delta x_1^{*,k}) \big) \end{aligned}\]
  • Implications
    • Ignoreing variance \(\implies\) underestimate welfare gain from feedback
    • Non-linearity of welfare gain: high bias \(\implies\) large \(\theta\) gain, large \(\Delta x_1^{*,k}\) change
