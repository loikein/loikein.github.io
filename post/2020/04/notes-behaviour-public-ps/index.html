Notes on Behavioural Public Policy - Problem Set

2020/04/15 Tags: notes micro public
  • Present Bias

Present Bias

BPP.PS1.Q1
An individual can choose how to distribute money across two time periods, \(t = 1, 2\) for consumption \(c_1\) and \(c_2\).
The total amount of money available is \(m\).
For every dollar not spent in period 1, the individual receives an interest rate \(r\).
Therefore, the overall budget constraint is given by (1): \[c_1 + c_2\cdot\frac{1}{1+r} = m\] Individuals have \((\beta, \delta)\) preferences.
The period utility functions are given by (2): \[u(c) = \frac{c^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho}\]

  1. For the ﬁrst two questions, assume that the individual can decide his consumption in periods 1 and 2 one period ahead in period 0.
    Deﬁne the maximization problem of the individual.
    Hint : subsitute \(c_2\) from the objective function by using the budget constraint (1).
  2. Derive the ﬁrst-order condition for optimal consumption and interpret it.
    Why does \(\beta\) not appear in the ﬁrst-order condition?
  3. Derive the equation for \(\Delta\ln c \equiv \ln c_2 − \ln c_1\), using the ﬁrst-order condition from (b).
  1. From \(t=0\), total utility function: \[\begin{aligned}U &= \beta\delta\cdot u(c_1) + \beta\delta^2\cdot u(c_2) \\ &= \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot\frac{c_2^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \end{aligned}\] Therefore, the individual’s problem is \[\begin{aligned}\max_{c_1} && &U = \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot\frac{c_2^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \\ s.t. && &c_1 + c_2\cdot\frac{1}{1+r} = m \end{aligned}\]
  2. Plug in \(c_2 = m-(1+r)c_1\): \[U = \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot\frac{\big(m-(1+r)c_1\big)^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \] FOC \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial U}{\partial c_1} &= \beta\delta\cdot c_1^{-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot (r-1)\big(m - (1-r)c_1\big)^{-\rho} \overset{!}{=}0\\ c_1^{-\rho} &= -\delta\cdot (r-1)\big(m - (1-r)c_1\big)^{-\rho} \\ c_1 &= -\delta^{\rho} \cdot(r-1)^{\rho}\cdot c_2 \\ \frac{c_1}{c_2} &= \big(\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho} \end{aligned}\] The condition means that the ratio of consumption in periods 1 and 2 should equalise to the combined time effect (\(\delta\) and interest rate).
    \(\beta\) does not appear because from the perspective of period 0, the individual acts like a TC who does not consider \(\beta\).
  3. From (b), \[\begin{aligned}\Delta\ln c &= \ln c_2 − \ln c_1 \\ &= \ln \frac{c_2}{c_1} \\ &= -\ln \big(\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho} \\ &= -\rho\big( \ln \delta + \ln (1-r) \big) \\ &= -\rho( \ln \delta - \ln r ) \\ &= \rho( \ln r - \ln \delta ) \end{aligned}\]
  1. Now characterize the optimal choice if an individual makes choices in in period 1.
    Deﬁne the objective function for the maximization problem.
    Characterize the ﬁrst-order condition and interpret it.
    Explain how it differs from the ﬁrst-order condition in (b).
  2. Derive the equation for \(\Delta\ln c \equiv \ln c_2 − \ln c_1\), using the ﬁrst-order condition from (d).
  1. From \(t=1\), the individual’s problem is \[\begin{aligned}\max_{c_1} && U &= u(c_1) + \beta\delta\cdot u(c_2) \\ && &= \frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_2^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \\ s.t. && c_1& + c_2\cdot\frac{1}{1+r} = m\end{aligned}\] Plug in \(c_2 = m-(1+r)c_1\) & FOC: \[\begin{aligned}U &= \frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta\cdot\frac{\big(m-(1+r)c_1\big)^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \\ \frac{\partial U}{\partial c_1} &= c_1^{-\rho} + \beta\delta\cdot (r-1)\big(m - (1-r)c_1\big)^{-\rho} \overset{!}{=}0\\ c_1 &= -(\beta\delta)^{\rho} \cdot(r-1)^{\rho}\cdot c_2 \\ \frac{c_1}{c_2} &= \big(\beta\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho}\end{aligned}\] This condition is different to that from (b), since from the perspective of period 1, the individual is present biased, and takes \(\beta\) into consideration.
  2. From (d), \[\begin{aligned}\Delta\ln c &= \ln c_2 − \ln c_1 \\ &= \ln \frac{c_2}{c_1} \\ &= -\ln \big(\beta\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho} \\ &= -\rho\big( \ln\beta + \ln\delta + \ln (1-r) \big) \\ &= -\rho(\ln\beta + \ln \delta - \ln r ) \\ &= \rho( \ln r -\ln\beta - \ln \delta ) \end{aligned}\]
  1. Suppose that 100 individuals make choices for \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) for various interest rates \(r = 0, 0.2, 0.5, 1\), both in period 0 and in period 1.
    How could you use these observations to estimate \(\beta\) and \(\delta\) from differences in consumption \(\Delta\ln c = \ln c_2 − \ln c_1\)?
    Write down the regression equation.
    Deﬁne and interpret each of the coefﬁcients.
  2. Assume that all individuals have the \(\rho\).
    Is it possible to estimate a separate \(\beta_i\) and \(\delta_i\) for each of the invididuals?
    Why would this be interesting?
  1. Set up regression function that \[\begin{aligned}\Delta\ln c &= \rho( \ln r -\ln\beta - \ln \delta ) \\ &= \underbrace{\beta_1}_{\rho}\cdot \ln r + \underbrace{\beta_2}_{-\rho\cdot\ln \beta} + \underbrace{\beta_3}_{-\rho\cdot\ln \delta}\cdot 1(t=1) \end{aligned}\]
