Present Bias

BPP.PS1.Q1
An individual can choose how to distribute money across two time periods, \(t = 1, 2\) for consumption \(c_1\) and \(c_2\).
The total amount of money available is \(m\).
For every dollar not spent in period 1, the individual receives an interest rate \(r\).
Therefore, the overall budget constraint is given by (1): \[c_1 + c_2\cdot\frac{1}{1+r} = m\] Individuals have \((\beta, \delta)\) preferences.
The period utility functions are given by (2): \[u(c) = \frac{c^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho}\]

  1. For the ﬁrst two questions, assume that the individual can decide his consumption in periods 1 and 2 one period ahead in period 0.
    Deﬁne the maximization problem of the individual.
    Hint : subsitute \(c_2\) from the objective function by using the budget constraint (1).
  2. Derive the ﬁrst-order condition for optimal consumption and interpret it.
    Why does \(\beta\) not appear in the ﬁrst-order condition?
  3. Derive the equation for \(\Delta\ln c \equiv \ln c_2 − \ln c_1\), using the ﬁrst-order condition from (b).
  1. From \(t=0\), total utility function: \[\begin{aligned}U &= \beta\delta\cdot u(c_1) + \beta\delta^2\cdot u(c_2) \\ &= \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot\frac{c_2^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \end{aligned}\] Therefore, the individual’s problem is \[\begin{aligned}\max_{c_1} && &U = \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot\frac{c_2^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \\ s.t. && &c_1 + c_2\cdot\frac{1}{1+r} = m \end{aligned}\]
  2. Plug in \(c_2 = m-(1+r)c_1\): \[U = \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot\frac{\big(m-(1+r)c_1\big)^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \] FOC \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial U}{\partial c_1} &= \beta\delta\cdot c_1^{-\rho} + \beta\delta^2\cdot (r-1)\big(m - (1-r)c_1\big)^{-\rho} \overset{!}{=}0\\ c_1^{-\rho} &= -\delta\cdot (r-1)\big(m - (1-r)c_1\big)^{-\rho} \\ c_1 &= -\delta^{\rho} \cdot(r-1)^{\rho}\cdot c_2 \\ \frac{c_1}{c_2} &= \big(\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho} \end{aligned}\] The condition means that the ratio of consumption in periods 1 and 2 should equalise to the combined time effect (\(\delta\) and interest rate).
    \(\beta\) does not appear because from the perspective of period 0, the individual acts like a TC who does not consider \(\beta\).
  3. From (b), \[\begin{aligned}\Delta\ln c &= \ln c_2 − \ln c_1 \\ &= \ln \frac{c_2}{c_1} \\ &= -\ln \big(\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho} \\ &= -\rho\big( \ln \delta + \ln (1-r) \big) \\ &= -\rho( \ln \delta - \ln r ) \\ &= \rho( \ln r - \ln \delta ) \end{aligned}\]
  1. Now characterize the optimal choice if an individual makes choices in in period 1.
    Deﬁne the objective function for the maximization problem.
    Characterize the ﬁrst-order condition and interpret it.
    Explain how it differs from the ﬁrst-order condition in (b).
  2. Derive the equation for \(\Delta\ln c \equiv \ln c_2 − \ln c_1\), using the ﬁrst-order condition from (d).
  1. From \(t=1\), the individual’s problem is \[\begin{aligned}\max_{c_1} && U &= u(c_1) + \beta\delta\cdot u(c_2) \\ && &= \frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta\cdot\frac{c_2^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \\ s.t. && c_1& + c_2\cdot\frac{1}{1+r} = m\end{aligned}\] Plug in \(c_2 = m-(1+r)c_1\) & FOC: \[\begin{aligned}U &= \frac{c_1^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} + \beta\delta\cdot\frac{\big(m-(1+r)c_1\big)^{1-\rho}}{1-\rho} \\ \frac{\partial U}{\partial c_1} &= c_1^{-\rho} + \beta\delta\cdot (r-1)\big(m - (1-r)c_1\big)^{-\rho} \overset{!}{=}0\\ c_1 &= -(\beta\delta)^{\rho} \cdot(r-1)^{\rho}\cdot c_2 \\ \frac{c_1}{c_2} &= \big(\beta\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho}\end{aligned}\] This condition is different to that from (b), since from the perspective of period 1, the individual is present biased, and takes \(\beta\) into consideration.
  2. From (d), \[\begin{aligned}\Delta\ln c &= \ln c_2 − \ln c_1 \\ &= \ln \frac{c_2}{c_1} \\ &= -\ln \big(\beta\delta\cdot(1-r)\big)^{\rho} \\ &= -\rho\big( \ln\beta + \ln\delta + \ln (1-r) \big) \\ &= -\rho(\ln\beta + \ln \delta - \ln r ) \\ &= \rho( \ln r -\ln\beta - \ln \delta ) \end{aligned}\]
  1. Suppose that 100 individuals make choices for \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) for various interest rates \(r = 0, 0.2, 0.5, 1\), both in period 0 and in period 1.
    How could you use these observations to estimate \(\beta\) and \(\delta\) from differences in consumption \(\Delta\ln c = \ln c_2 − \ln c_1\)?
    Write down the regression equation.
    Deﬁne and interpret each of the coefﬁcients.
  2. Assume that all individuals have the \(\rho\).
    Is it possible to estimate a separate \(\beta_i\) and \(\delta_i\) for each of the invididuals?
    Why would this be interesting?
  1. Set up regression function that \[\begin{aligned}\Delta\ln c &= \rho( \ln r -\ln\beta - \ln \delta ) \\ &= \underbrace{\beta_1}_{\rho}\cdot \ln r + \underbrace{\beta_2}_{-\rho\cdot\ln \beta} + \underbrace{\beta_3}_{-\rho\cdot\ln \delta}\cdot 1(t=1) + \epsilon \end{aligned}\]
  2. Yes, just add \(i\) to \(\beta_2\), \(\beta_3\) and \(\epsilon\)

Optimal Taxation

BPP.PS1.Q2
An individual can consume three goods \(x_1, x_2, x_3\).
Good \(x_1\) has a utility cost \(c\) per unit that occurs one period later.
There are three periods.
In period 0, the government sets taxes by choosing the socially optimal tax rate \(t^*\).
In period 1, individuals are making consumption choices.
In period 2, no decisions are taken, but the utility cost from consumption of \(x_1\) described above occurs. The utility function viewed from period 1 is given by (3): \[U = u(x_1) + v(x_2) + x_3 -\beta\delta c\cdot x_1\] where \(u(\cdot)\) and \(v(\cdot)\) are concave functions with \(u'(x_1) > 0\), \(u''(x_1)<0\) and \(v'(x_2)>0\), \(v''(x_2) < 0\).
Notice that the problem is slightly simpler as in class, since utility is separable between \(x_1\) and \(x_2\).
In addtition, \(u'''(x_1)>0\) (this information will be useful for question (f) below).
The budget constraint is \[z\geq (p_1 + t)x_1 + p_2x_2 + x_3\] However, not all individuals are the same with respect to time preferences.
All individuals have the same exponential discount factor \(\delta\).
A fraction \(\theta\) of individuals is present biased and has \(\beta < 1\).
The remaining fraction \(1 − \theta\) is time consistent and consequently has \(\beta = 1\).

  1. Characterize the optimal choices of \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) using the ﬁrst-order conditions for both types of individuals.
    How do their choices differ?
  2. Characterize graphically the optimal choice of \(x_1\) for both individuals in one graph.
    Show how \(\beta\) affects the consumption of present-biased individuals.
  1. Plug in budget to utility function: \[\begin{aligned}U &= u(x_1) + v(x_2) + x_3 -\beta\delta c\cdot x_1 \\ &= u(x_1)-\beta\delta c\cdot x_1 - (p_1 + t)x_1 + v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z\end{aligned}\] FOC: \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_1} &= u_1 - \beta\delta c - (p_1 + t) \overset{!}{=} 0\\ u_1 &= \beta\delta c + p_1 + t \\ \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_2} &= v_2 - p_2 \overset{!}{=} 0 \\ v_2 &= p_2 \end{aligned}\]
    1. For PB, \(\beta < 1\): \[\begin{cases}u_1 = \beta\delta c + p_1 + t \\ v_2 = p_2 \end{cases}\]
    2. For TC, \(\beta = 1\): \[\begin{cases}u_1 =\delta c + p_1 + t \\ v_2 = p_2 \end{cases}\]
  2. See pic

  1. The government think that \(\beta\) should be entering the welfare function for the reasons we discussed in class.
    Therefore, the government does not consider \(\beta\) for discounting between periods 0 and 1.
    Derive the aggregate welfare function: \[W(t) = \theta W(t)^{PB} + (1-\theta)W(t)^{TC}\] Where \(W(t)^{PB}\) and \(W(t)^{TC}\) are welfare for each group of agents.
    Derive the internality \(\gamma\) that we discussed for each of the groups.
  2. Derive the welfare effects of taxation \(\frac{\partial W(t)}{\partial t}\).
    Discuss how it is different from the expression obtained in class.
  3. Derive the optimal tax rate \(t^*\) in terms of \(\theta\), \(\gamma\), \(\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t}\) and \(\frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t}\).
    How does it vary with \(\theta\)?
  4. Show that \(t^* > \theta\delta(1 − \beta)c\).
    Use a graphical argument based on the graph in questions (b) above.
    Explain the interprettation.
    Hint: What does \(u''' (x_1) > 0\) imply for the shape of \(u'(x_1)\)?
    1. For PB, \[\begin{aligned}W(t)^{PB} &= U^{PB} + tx_1^{PB} + \beta\delta c\cdot x_1^{PB} - \delta c\cdot x_1^{PB} \\ &= U^{PB} + tx_1^{PB} - (1-\beta)\cdot\delta c\cdot x_1^{PB} \\ &= u(x_1)-\beta\delta c\cdot x_1 - (p_1 + t)x_1 \\ &\phantom{\ggg}+ v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z + tx_1 - (1-\beta)\cdot\delta c\cdot x_1\\ &= u(x_1) - p_1 x_1 + v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z - \delta c\cdot x_1 \\ &= u(x_1) - x_1(p_1 + \delta c) + v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z\end{aligned}\] FOC \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W(t)^{PB}}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial u(x_1)}{\partial x_1}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} -(p_1 + \delta c)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} \\ &= (\beta\delta c + p_1 + t - p_1 - \delta c)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} \\ &= \big(t - \underbrace{(1-\beta)\delta c}_{\gamma}\big)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\]
    2. For TC, \[\begin{aligned}W(t)^{TC} &= U^{TC} + tx_1^{TC} \\ &= u(x_1)- \delta c\cdot x_1 - (p_1 + t)x_1 + v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z + tx_1 \\ &= u(x_1) - p_1x_1 -\delta c\cdot x_1 + v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z \\ &= u(x_1) - x_1(p_1 - \delta c)+ v(x_2) - p_2x_2 + z\end{aligned}\] FOC \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W(t)^{TC}}{\partial t} &= \frac{\partial u(x_1)}{\partial x_1}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} -(p_1 + \delta c)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} \\ &= (\delta c + p_1 + t - p_1 - \delta c)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t} \\ &= t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial t}\end{aligned}\] where internality is zero.
  2. Adding PB and TC together, \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W(t)}{\partial t} &= \theta\cdot\big(t - (1-\beta)\delta c\big)\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} +(1-\theta)\cdot t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} \\ &= \theta\cdot(t - \gamma^{PB})\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} +(1-\theta)\cdot t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\] It contains two parts because there are two groups of people, whose \(\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\) differ from each other’s.
  3. Set FOC for total welfare to zero \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial W(t)}{\partial t} &= \theta\cdot(t - \gamma^{PB})\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} +(1-\theta)\cdot t\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} \overset{!}{=} 0 \\ \theta\cdot\gamma^{PB}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} &= \Big(\theta\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} + (1-\theta)\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} \Big)t^* \\ t^* &= \frac{\theta\cdot\gamma^{PB}\cdot(\partial x_1^{PB}/ \partial t)}{\theta\cdot (\partial x_1^{PB}/ \partial t) + (1-\theta)\cdot (\partial x_1^{TC}/ \partial t)} \\ &=\theta\cdot(1-\beta)\delta c\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}/ \partial t}{\theta\cdot (\partial x_1^{PB}/ \partial t) + (1-\theta)\cdot (\partial x_1^{TC}/ \partial t)} \end{aligned}\]
  4. Since \(x_1^{PB} > x_1^{TC}\), \(u'''>0\), \(u''<0\) \[u''(x_1^{TC}) < u''(x_1^{PB})<0\] Also, we have \[\begin{aligned}1 &= \frac{\partial u'(x_1^{PB})}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial u'(x_1^{TC})}{\partial t} \\ &= \frac{\partial u'(x_1^{PB})}{\partial x_1^{PB}}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} \\ &= \frac{\partial u'(x_1^{TC})}{\partial x_1^{TC}}\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} \\ u''(x_1^{PB})\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} &= u''(x_1^{TC})\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} \\ \frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} &< \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} <0\end{aligned}\] From (e), it is sufficient to show that \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\overbrace{\partial x_1^{PB}/ \partial t}^{<0}}{\underbrace{\theta\cdot (\partial x_1^{PB}/ \partial t) + (1-\theta)\cdot (\partial x_1^{TC}/ \partial t)}_{<0}} &> 1\\ \theta\cdot\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} + (1-\theta)\cdot \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} &> \frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} \\ \frac{\partial x_1^{TC}}{\partial t} &>\frac{\partial x_1^{PB}}{\partial t} \end{aligned}\] End of proof.

Salience Bias

BPP.PS2.Q1
There are three goods: air conditioning use in kWh (\(x_1\)), time spent napping at home (\(x_2\)), and a numeraire good with constant marginal utility of consumption and price 1 per unit of the good.
Air conditioning costs \(p_1\) per hour of air conditioning use.
Napping is free. The individual has a total budget of \(z\) available for consumption.
However, air conditioning use is not easily visible and the individual has salience bias.
The consumer only perceives a fraction \(\theta\) of the cost of air conditioning use when choosing consumption.
The individual chooses \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) to maximize (1): \[U= u(x_1,x_2) + z - \theta\cdot p_1x_1\] where \(u(x_1,x_2)\) is given by: \[u(x_1,x_2) = \alpha_1 x_1 - \frac{1}{2}x_1^2 + \alpha_2 x_2 - \frac{1}{2}x_2^2 + \gamma x_1 x_2\] where \(\alpha_1,\alpha_2>0\), \(0<\gamma <1\).

  1. Derive the ﬁrst-order conditions for the choices of the individual maximizing the objective function (1).
  2. Solve for the optimal values \(x_1^*\) and \(x_2^*\).
    Explain what role salience bias plays in determining consumption.
    Explain the intuition behind the result that the optimal amount of napping also depends on \(\theta\).
  3. Calculate \(\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta}\) and \(\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p_1}\).
    Interpret their signs and explain role of \(\gamma\).
    What happens when \(\gamma = 0\) compared to when \(\gamma > 0\)?
  1. FOC for \(u(x_1,x_2)\): \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x_1} &= \alpha_1 - x_1 + \gamma x_2 \\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x_2} &= \alpha_2 - x_2 + \gamma x_1 \end{aligned}\] therefore, for \(U\): \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_1} &= \alpha_1 - x_1 + \gamma x_2 - \theta p \\ \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_2} &= \alpha_2 - x_2 + \gamma x_1 \end{aligned}\]
  2. According to (a), \[\begin{cases}\alpha_1 - x_1 + \gamma x_2 - \theta p \overset{!}{=} 0\\ \alpha_2 - x_2 + \gamma x_1 \overset{!}{=} 0 \end{cases}\] therefore, \[\begin{aligned}x_1^* &= \frac{\alpha_1 + \gamma\alpha_2 - \theta p}{1-\gamma^2}\\ x_2^* &= \frac{\alpha_2 + \gamma\alpha_1 - \gamma\theta p}{1-\gamma^2} \end{aligned}\] Salience bias \(\theta\) negatively affects the consumption of both goods, by different portions.
    \(x_2^*\) depends on \(\theta\) because the utility of \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) are inter-dependent through term \(\gamma x_1 x_2\).
  3. From (b), \[\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{-p}{1-\gamma^2} <0 \\ \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial p} &= \frac{-\theta}{1-\gamma^2} <0 \end{aligned}\]
