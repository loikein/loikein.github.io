1.2 Notes from interview tutorial

  • Don’t fear to ask for repetition
    • Do you mind rephrasing the question?
  • Pay attention to their facial expression
    • Did that answered your question?
    • Do you need further clarification?
  • Answer to general questions should be 1 ~ 2 min long
  • Only talk about UK schools: Eidingburg, LSE, UCL
    • Ongoing research, faculty,
  • What faculties are you interested in
    • Mention a paper they work together

1.2.1 Why do you choose this school

  • 2 ~ 3 points, related to the interviewers
  • Curriculum: courses, research focused
  • Ongoing research in the school

1.2.2 Your question

  • Show you’re trully interested
    • When would I know about my supervisor
    • How engaged the supervisors are? What supervision looks like?
      • Laure was awarded the Teaching Excellence Award for PhD supervision in 2017, and the Teaching Excellence Award for undergraduate teaching in 2018.
    • Cir-related questions
    • No question about life & stuff
  • Can I talk to current PhD students?

1.3 TO-DO

Review your research proposal or statement of purpose.

  • The interviewer will likely make reference to it during the interview.
  • Go over the experiences that have prepared you for a PhD and be ready to give specific examples during the interview.
  • Be able to explain the reasons why you applied to this program in particular.

Be prepared to talk about your research interests in detail.

  • Show that you have put some thought into what you wrote.
  • Show that you have the required background knowledge, including knowledge of the key people in your research area, methodologies you plan to use, or studies you want to reference.

Think about your motivation for pursuing a PhD.

  • The interviewers want to know you have put some thought into the decision to pursue a PhD.
  • They also want to gauge your commitment to the project before they invest time and money in you.
  • Think about how a PhD will help you achieve your career goals.

Read your potential supervisor’s work.

  • Show you are serious about working with them.
  • Demonstrate why you want to work with them in particular and how their expertise will be essential to your research.
  • If you are interviewing for a small program, familiarize yourself with the work (or at least the fields of expertise) of the other faculty members.

Familiarize yourself with current scholarship in the field.

  • Demonstrate your engagement with field and that you can think critically about the current debates.
  • You should know how your proposed research will fit into the current scholarship and what makes it unique.

1.4 On Skype Interview

  • Strong internet connection
  • Check your audio and video feed
  • Close other programs in your computer
  • Make sure your roommates are aware of when your interview is and understand not to disturb you during the interview
  • Well-lit area with a tidy, neutral background.
  • If possible, face a natural light source.
  • Place your computer on top of a pile of books so that the camera is at eye level
  • Look at the camera when you speak to make “eye contact” with the interviewer
  • Print your notes instead of reading them from the computer

1.5 Possible Questions

1.5.1 About You

  • Tell us about yourself…
    • Background, interests, relate to your academic specialism and your choice of university.
    • Something specifically inspired you to consider a PhD
    • Something that’s attracted you to this city as well as the university

    My Answer
    My father was a professor in sociology before he retired, and my mom is a librarian at the same university. Since I was a kid, I kind of knew that I am going to go through an academic path. Also, I would love to experience life in different places, so I joined Waseda University in Japan, and later here at Bonn University in Germany.
    During my study, I mainly studied microeconomics like game theory and contract theory. I also took many maths, econometrics & programming courses, trying to get used to different methods, because I gradually realised that if I want to continue my study, these knowledge are pretty much needed.

  • What made you choose to do a PhD?
    • Motivation and drive through years of hard work
    • You’re doing the PhD for the right reasons, and that you’ll still care about it in two years

    My Answer
    I’ve enjoyed the classes so far, but I feel I could do more given the chance to work as an researcher. Being a PhD student means I can engage more closely in the research discussion than I ever could as a undergrad/master student.

  • What do you plan to do after you complete your PhD?
    • Academic career paths
    • Some thought into alternatives

    My Answer
    Teaching has been attractive to me for a long time, so I am looking at possiblities like becoming a lecturer at some university. Proceeding to postdoc and being research-focused also sounds great, dependes on the chances appear at the time.
    But also, since what I want is essentially to do some contribution to the society, there is no limit as where can I do that. So, I am also open to maybe positions like becoming a researcher in the industry, after all, that is the place where the innovations can finally land.

  • What are your strengths and weaknesses?
    • Solid examples for strengths and showing how they relate to the PhD project you have in mind.
    • When you’ve had to be well organised and show that you understand the importance of self-directed study to a successful PhD.
    • Things you find challenging, but identify them as training needs and say how you expect to improve upon them as part of your PhD.

    My Answer
    I am extremely organised, for example, I can still find almost every note and file that I used since undergraduate study, as well as most of the references entries I cited in class papers.
    However, I cannot remember & recall important papers/people’s names, which sometimes makes me less persuasive than I could have been. I am hoping that more research input and presentation opportunities offered in this programme would help me on that.

  • Are there any training needs you can identify ahead of your PhD?
    • Aims and aspirations as a researcher
    • Show that you’ve read up on the project and programme you’re applying to.
    • The university’s training modules
    • What you hope to gain from them and how you think they’ll help you succeed in your PhD
    • The transferrable skills this PhD can give you.
    • Gaps in the core skills required by your discipline

    My Answer
    As you have probably noticed, there is a gap between my previous training and my research interest. On the one hand, maybe I could provide some unique perspectives from a slightly different background, but on the other hand, I really need to catch up with the common practice in public economics and health economics, so the courses in the first years should be great for that.

1.5.2 About Your PhD project

  • Why this project?
    • Show you’ve made an informed choice and are enthusiastic enough to see it through.
    • Show passion and enthusiasm for your research proposal, discuss and expand upon it.
    • Existing scholarship and show an awareness of the field
    • What makes your project distinctive

    My Answer
    I have been interested in topics related to the society for a long time, but the project, its offers, and the faculty members were really what encouraged me to submit my research proposal. I would be given the chance to work with highly experienced people, and to do the research I always wanted to do.
    Especially the …

  • What makes you the right candidate for this PhD?
    • Your previous work at undergraduate or Masters level, show passion and give examples.
    • If your Masters has given you skills in exactly the kind of statistical analysis required by this doctorate, mention that.

    EAnswer
    I’ve been interested in this topic since the final year of my undergraduate degree. This lead to my choice of Masters and helped me pick my dissertation topic, which I really enjoyed. I’m really excited to now go on and do some sustained research in this area as a PhD student.

  • What difficulties do you expect to encounter during this project?
    • You’ve put some thought into the approach necessary for your research and the methodology you might use.
    • Problems you aren’t yet certain how to solve, but suggest how you might go about investigating them.
    • Training needs, how you plan to take advantage of development opportunities within your programme.

    EAnswer
    I can see that some of the archival material I’ll need to examine for this project may be difficult to access. My first task will be to request permissions, arrange visits and develop a system for recording my findings. I’m hoping to undertake training in archival practices and seek advice from my supervisor as I develop these key skills early in my project.

  • What would you like the impact of this project to be?
    • What you hope the wider outcome of your project might be
    • The ‘outputs’ from your project, educational workshops, policy guidance, etc.
    • wWays in which you could take part in public engagement

    EAnswer
    I’m keen to share my passion for this subject with a wider audience. I’m hoping to maintain a public-facing blog documenting my research and would also be keen to approach local schools and museums to discuss educational events.

  • How will you fund this project?
    • It’s fine to say that you’ll be looking for extra funding and part-time work as you start the project, but make it clear that you’ll still have enough time to apply yourself to the actual research.

    EAnswer
    I’ve shortlisted external funders and would be keen to investigate any small bursaries or other forms of support through the university. I’ve also made arrangements to work part-time, with the option to adjust this if my funding situation improves.

1.5.3 About the Choice of University

  • Why have you chosen to study a PhD at this university?
    • Why you’d like to study with these supervisors
    • When you’ve used their work during your Bachelors or Masters
    • How you can contribute to their future projects
    • Other departments or laboratories are undertaking related work
    • aRelevant research facilities, resources or collections

    EAnswer
    I’ve looked at lots of opportunities in this area. I feel that this project is the best of its kind, combining a unique research angle with a training programme that will meet my professional needs. I was already familiar with the work of my prospective supervisor and their research has greatly informed my own development as a scholar. I’m eager to combine my work with theirs and make use of the facilities the university has put together for this project.

  • What can you bring to this research group?
    • Your experience (academic or professional) and outline your ambitions
    • You will make the most of the proposed project and provide a return

    EAnswer
    I’m eager to take advantage of the facilities and expertise this university has to offer. But I also want to contribute with my own expertise and enthusiasm. My previous work has given me the skills to make the most of the material involved in this project and I’m motivated to take up new training opportunities as relevant. I’ll be proud to be a part of this department and would actively seek to represent it through my own publications and other research outputs.

1.6 Your Own questions

  • Things that would concern you as a PhD student
  • Library resources and lab facilities
  • Avoid asking for information that’s readily available elsewhere

1.6.2 About the School

  • What sort of resources does the university have? (Libraries, lab equipment etc.)
  • What do they do to promote work/life balance?
  • Is the university or department likely to run any events or other associated projects during the period of my PhD?

1.6.3 About the Supervisor

  • What will the supervision arrangements be for the project?
  • How does your potential supervisor mentor students?
  • What can your potential mentor/supervisor do to advance your career?
  • How many other PhD students has this supervisor seen to completion?

1.6.4 About the Programme

  • What kind of training and skills sessions are offered as part of the PhD programme?
  • Will I have opportunities to teach / present / publish?
  • Are there likely to be any changes to the funding arrangements for the project?

1.6.5 Towards & After Graduation

  • What is the program’s average time to degree?
  • What is the program’s job placement record?
